An ADR captures one load-bearing decision: the situation that motivated it, the concrete choice, and what it enables, obligates, or forecloses. The log is append-only — decisions never get edited in place; they are **superseded** by later ADRs.

## How to read an ADR

Each page uses the same template:

<Steps>
  <Step title="Status line">
    An `<Info>` block near the top gives the current status (`Accepted`, `Superseded`, `Deprecated`) and the date. `Supersedes` and `Superseded by` appear here when relevant.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Context">
    The situation and constraints that motivated the decision. Two to four paragraphs — no more.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Decision">
    The concrete choice, stated as an imperative. This is the sentence that appears in the page's meta `description` field.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Consequences">
    What the decision **enables**, **obligates**, or **forecloses**. The second-order effects the author was trading off.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Related">
    Links back to the Reference, Platform, and other Decision pages the ADR touches. Where a spec is split across multiple Mintlify pages, every target is listed.
  </Step>
</Steps>

## When to write a new ADR

Write one when:

- A choice locks in infrastructure (runtime, framework, persistence model).
- The decision will be hard to reverse without cascading rewrites.
- Two or more reasonable positions exist and the choice needs explanation.
- A future maintainer would ask "why did we do it this way?" and the answer isn't obvious from the code or spec.

Do **not** write one for implementation details already clear from the spec, style or lint choices, or scope-cutting decisions (those go in the [Roadmap](/concepts/roadmap)).

Keep each ADR to ~200 words. If it needs more, it probably contains more than one decision — split it.

## Numbering

ADRs are numbered monotonically. Never renumber, never delete — **supersede** instead. The current log runs from ADR-001 through ADR-053.
