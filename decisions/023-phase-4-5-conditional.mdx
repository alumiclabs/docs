## Context

The roadmap ([Roadmap](/concepts/roadmap)) lists five phases:

- Phase 1: Kernel + first template (6-8 weeks).
- Phase 2: ECS + assets + state + save (8-10 weeks).
- Phase 3: Flow + ability + dialogue + multiplayer (4-6 weeks).
- Phase 4: Editor, canvas, UI designer, vector editor (ongoing).
- Phase 5: bitECS + WebGPU + Rust/WASM (as needed).

Phase 4 is "ongoing" and Phase 5 is "as needed" — but nothing in the spec says whether they are committed deliverables or conditional investments. A reader plans around "Phase 5 is coming" or "Phase 5 will never ship" without a clear signal.

## Decision

Alumic is considered **feature-complete when Phase 3 verification passes**. Phases 4 and 5 are **opt-in investments**, shipped incrementally based on adoption signal:

- **Phase 4** (editor + creative tools + marketplace) ships if there is clear demand for visual authoring. Milestones are incremental — one creative tool at a time, each verified independently. Risk: teams adopting Alumic for Phase 1-3 features get editors they didn't need. Acceptance: editor mode can be disabled with zero cost in pure-runtime deployments.

- **Phase 5** (advanced backends: bitECS, WebGPU, Rust/WASM, server-authoritative multiplayer, CRDT collab) ships in response to specific performance or capability needs. If no adopter hits those ceilings, Phase 5 may never ship. Each Phase 5 deliverable has its own "done when" criterion when it enters the queue.

The roadmap's Risk Mitigation section documents this stance explicitly.

## Consequences

**Enables:** Flexibility to respond to real signal rather than committing to features speculatively. MVP discipline — ship what the market needs, not what the research suggested.

**Obligates:** The spec must be explicit that Phase 1-3 is the committed scope. Marketing and community comms must not over-promise Phase 4/5. Early adopters know what they're buying into.

**Forecloses:** A rigid "we will ship all five phases" roadmap. Engineering capacity in year two goes where demand points.
